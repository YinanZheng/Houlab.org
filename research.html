Research Interest

The overarching research focus of the Population Epigenetics Laboratory is to understand the biological mechanisms and identify biomarkers linking environmental and behavioral lifestyle factors with subclinical or clinical disease development, with the ultimate goal of developing effective strategies based on these findings for early detection and prevention as well as clinical intervention and treatment of human diseases. The environmental/lifestyle risk factors we have studied include air pollution, pesticides, being overweight or physically inactive, and diet/nutrition as well as prenatal exposures. Our primary focus is on cancer, but our research has involved a variety of other chronic diseases and outcomes including cardiovascular and respiratory diseases, obesity, and diabetes mellitus in adults and/or neonates/children.

The integration of molecular, bioinformatics, and advanced statistical techniques into epidemiology offers new insights into environmental and lifestyle factors that affect genetic susceptibility to and risk of getting diseases. Recent technological advances provide an enormous amount of “omic” data, from whole-genome sequencing to extensive transcriptomic, methylomic, and metabolomic data. We have used these data in conjunction with our expert team to investigate genetic factors (i.e., mutations/polymorphisms, telomere shortening, and mitochondrial DNA variations); and are currently focusing on epigenetic factors (i.e., DNA methylation, histone modifications, and long non-coding RNA and microRNA profiles) which modify gene expression without changing the underlying DNA sequence. We have generated and analyzed abundant genome-wide epigenetic profiling data for several large cohort studies including the Coronary Artery Risk Development in Young Adults Study (CARDIA), the Women's Health Initiative (WHI), the Normative Aging Study (NAS), Project VIVA, and others.

We offer our expertise to Northwestern University and external physicians and scientists interested in incorporating epigenetic/genetic factors and methods into their research including observational studies, clinical trials, behavioral interventions, and others. Our group can consult on study design, bioinformatic computation, data analysis and interpretation, and biological sample collection schemes and assays. If you are interested in collaborating with Northwestern’s Population Epigenetics Lab on your research please contact Brian Joyce (b-joyce@northwestern.edu) or Tao Gao (tao.gao@northwestern.edu).
